The Dow and the Standard & Poors 500-stock index hit highs on Friday for a sixth consecutive week of gains, as investors continued to take cues from the nominee to lead the Federal Reserve, Janet L. Yellen, who told a Senate committee it was too early to end the central banks stimulus.

Shares of Exxon Mobil led the blue-chip Dow index higher. They rose 2.2 percent to $95.27 a day after Berkshire Hathaway, controlled by Warren E. Buffett, disclosed a new $3.45 billion stake in the company, the second-largest American company by market value, behind only Apple.

Both the S.& P. and the Dow capped a fourth consecutive day of gains, and the S.& P. 500 finished within two points of 1,800.

I think there is a general expectation that the market is going to continue to rally for the rest of the year, said Brad McMillan, chief investment officer for Commonwealth Financial, based in Waltham, Mass.

Toward the end of the year, fund managers may help bolster stocks as they chase performance.

I think its going to be a slow grind up, said Dan Veru, chief investment officer at Palisade Capital Management, adding that the only thing that can derail this is some exogenous macroeconomic event that comes out of nowhere.

The Dow Jones industrial average was up 85.48 points, or 0.54 percent, at 15,961.70. The S.& P. 500 index was up 7.56 points, or 0.42 percent, at 1,798.18. The Nasdaq composite index was up 13.23 points, or 0.33 percent, at 3,985.97.

A number of big hedge funds disclosed that they had taken positions in the ailing department store J. C. Penney, sending its shares up 3.9 percent to $9.03.

FedEx shares added 1.6 percent to $138.65 after filings showed that three hedge funds  Third Point, controlled by Daniel Loeb; Soros Fund Management, led by George Soros; and Paulson & Company, controlled by John Paulson  had taken stakes in the company.

Data showed that New York states manufacturing sector unexpectedly shrank this month while the nations industrial production fell in October as output fell at power plants and mines.

In the market for government bonds, the price of the benchmark 10-year Treasury note declined 3/32 to 100 13/32, and its yield edged up to 2.7 percent from 2.69 percent late Thursday.
